THIS WEEK IN CRYPTO

FTX Fire Sale

  • FTX one of the world’s largest crypto exchanges sold to competitor Binance today to shirk insolvency.
  • Yesterday FTX froze withdrawals of customer funds. Lack of demand for their own FTX Token, which they had ~$575M of, was one of a few culprits.
  • In January, FTX raised $400M from Paradigm at a $32B valuation.

The Rise of FTX

 

This summer, the press was calling FTX Founder Sam Bankman Fried (SBF) Crypto’s JP Morgan. He’d just bailed out Voyager Digital and BlockFi and was effectively crypto’s lender of last resort.

 

Started in 2018, FTX has scaled into one of the world’s largest crypto exchanges and most valuable companies in just four years.

 

In 2021 they bought the naming rights to the Miami Heat's stadium for $135 million. As recently as September, FTX was leading VC investments into startups like Aptos and Sui which had their own $1B+ valuations.

 

Not only did they raise $400M last January, but they also raised another $420M from Sequoia three months before. In July 2021 they raised $1B even.

 

Yesterday FTX was one of the world’s most valuable and well-funded startups.

 

As of today, FTX is gone.

FTX Founder Sam Bankman Fried

What Went Wrong?

 

Liquidity Crunch: It turns out that bailing out failing crypto exchanges costs a ton of money.

 

In a nutshell, FTX took on too many risky assets and when news came out it was short on cash investors panicked and all tried to withdraw their funds at once.

 

It was a run on the bank. Just like that classic scene from It’s A Wonderful Life.

Why Not To Invent Your Own Currency

 

FTX’s tragic flaw may have been that most of its assets were locked up in its own FTT token.

 

FTX had ~$5.75B in FTT. As the volume of crypto trading plummeted this year, that FTT got harder and harder to sell.

 

So while they technically had $5.75B, it was all locked up in FTX tokens no one wanted to buy. Which is a little like having nothing at all. This weekend investors picked up on that.

It’s been a tough 24 hours for FTX’s Token

Crypto is not always as liquid as we’d like to think. Sure, it’s extremely liquid when the price is going to the moon and everyone wants a piece. But for a niche token like FTT, in times like these when demand is low, it’s hard to sell a few million dollars worth without crashing the price.

 

That’s not a concern for a consumer like me, but for institutions that can be a death blow.

 

What’s most egregious is that FTX isn’t the first exchange to fall this way. SBF just bailed out two firms that collapsed under these exact circumstances. He should have known.

Binance V. FTX

 

The most interesting piece of the story is how Binance Founder CZ played this masterfully. Binance is the world’s largest crypto exchange by trading volume and now the proud owner of FTX.

 

Over the weekend CZ stoked fears FTX was going to collapse. Then he publicly announced Binance was going to sell $580M in FTT due to those concerns. Given no one was buying FTT a sale of that magnitude was undoubtedly going to crash the price, which sent investors into a panic and kicked off the run on FTX.

 

Today, he acquired FTX, one of his largest competitors, in a fire sale.

It was a stroke of capitalist genius to weaponize the mob against FTX and create a panic, but it’s also a nail in crypto’s coffin.

 

Binance is more than a little bit… sketchy I think is the technical term?

 

It's currently being investigated US Department of Justice and IRS for money laundering. UK financial regulators have ordered they stop operating in the UK but they have not. They operate worldwide and don’t pay much attention to regulations in any country. They don’t even have an official headquarters, to make it harder for regulators to pin them down.

 

They also built ties with the FSB by giving them detailed customer data including names and addresses.

 

Large institutions are not going to invest in crypto via Binance. That’s not good for the industry.

CZ and SBF after Binance’s 2017 investment into FTX

Lessons from 1929

 

In the years leading up to the Great Depression, nearly 2,000 banks in the United States failed. Collapsing asset prices and defaults after the run-up of the roaring ‘20s left banks over-extended and vulnerable to consumer panics.

 

Today, crypto is going through its own crash. Collapse after collapse of massive crypto exchanges has left crypto investors on edge, easily spooked by rumors of default. Since crypto prices have fallen off a cliff exchanges have little capital to appease investors when they all come calling at once.

 

The end of FTX is the worst thing to happen to crypto yet. If you can’t trust FTX who can you trust?

VC FUNDING

The Coterie

Raise: $40M Series A from Andreessen Horowitz

One Liner: Lending for startup founders

Liquidity is a surprisingly big problem for startup founders.

 

Imagine a founder who just raised a new venture round at a $500M valuation. Despite being rich on paper it’s likely they’d struggle to get a mortgage with just their company assets.

 

Banks aren't big fans of private startup valuations they care more about… money in the bank. Privately held companies are hard to get liquidity out of, as we just saw to stunning effect in the case of FTX.

 

Coterie is designed to solve that problem for founders. They lend against founders’ equity in private startups. As a bonus they let founders invest in venture funds along with hot private startups like Stripe.

 

It’s a cool idea but again, as we saw today, illiquid assets have to be carefully managed to avoid a liquidity crunch.
